Who should get fit?

While a fitting can help nearly all skill levels, a few exceptions matter. If you are unsure, read on or reach out before you book.

Golfers who should

  • Consistent enough contact. You do not need a tour swing—occasional mishits are normal. If you are generally making solid contact and not living with extreme tops, severe duck hooks on every swing, etc., you are in a good place to get fit. Those swing issues are usually better handled with lessons first.
  • Recently practicing or playing. If you have not hit a ball in six months, consider a few range sessions or rounds first so we fit your “in-season” swing—not a rusty move that may change after a week back in golf.
  • Working to improve. If you still have inconsistency but you are with a coach or practicing on a plan, we can still point you in a helpful direction; results may be less dramatic in one session, but the right specs pay off over time.

Golfers who should wait

  • Severe contact struggles. If you rarely get the ball airborne or fight extreme mishits every swing, lessons usually come first. We can still help with sizing for height, speed, and ability, but dial-in fitting works best once contact is more stable.

Types of fittings

Driver & woods: optimize launch, spin, smash, and ball speed while keeping you in play off the tee. Fairway woods bridge the gap to your longest iron or hybrid.

Hybrids: versatile gapping clubs—focus on landing angle, spin, and distance for long approaches and trouble shots.

Irons & wedges: lie, length, launch, landing angle, and spin so distances are predictable and wedges stop on the green.

Full bag: can be completed in one visit, but splitting into multiple sessions is recommended to avoid fatigue skewing results. Expect roughly three hours if you insist on a single marathon session.
